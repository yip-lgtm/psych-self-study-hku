# PSYC7306 — Personality Psychology

## Course Info

- **Code:** PSYC7306
- **Title:** Personality psychology
- **MSocSc(Psy) Compulsory:** Yes (one of 8)
- **BPsych Equiv:** PSYC2019
- **Self-Study Month:** 3

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this course, you should be able to:

1. Describe major personality theories (psychodynamic, trait, humanistic, social-cognitive)
2. Explain the Big Five (Five-Factor Model) and its evidence base
3. Discuss personality assessment methods (self-report, projective, behavioral)
4. Evaluate the stability of personality across the lifespan
5. Apply personality psychology to real-world contexts (work, relationships, health)
6. Discuss personality neuroscience and genetics
7. Critically evaluate the field's debates (e.g., person vs. situation)
8. Connect personality to other subfields (social, cognitive, clinical)

## Topic Outline

### Topic 1: Introduction to Personality Psychology (2 hours)
- What is personality?
- Person vs. situation debate
- Nomothetic vs. idiographic approaches
- Levels of analysis (dispositional, characteristic, situational)
- History of the field

### Topic 2: Psychodynamic Approach (5 hours)
- Freud's structure: id, ego, superego
- Psychosexual stages (oral, anal, phallic, latency, genital)
- Defense mechanisms (repression, projection, denial, sublimation)
- Neo-Freudians: Jung (collective unconscious, archetypes), Adler (inferiority complex), Horney
- Critiques (unscientific, unfalsifiable)
- Modern psychodynamic ideas (object relations, attachment)

### Topic 3: Trait Theory I — Origins (4 hours)
- Allport: cardinal, central, secondary traits
- Cattell: 16 Personality Factors (16PF)
- Eysenck: PEN model (Psychoticism, Extraversion, Neuroticism)
- Factor analysis logic
- Lexical hypothesis

### Topic 4: Trait Theory II — Big Five (5 hours)
- Five-Factor Model (FFM): O, C, E, A, N
- Costa & McCrae NEO-PI-R
- IPIP-NEO-120 (free alternative)
- Evidence: cross-cultural, cross-language, predictive validity
- HEXACO (adds Honesty-Humility)
- Big Five and behavior: real-life correlates

### Topic 5: Humanistic Approach (3 hours)
- Rogers: self-concept, unconditional positive regard
- Maslow: hierarchy of needs
- Self-actualization
- Critiques (naive, untestable)
- Positive psychology legacy

### Topic 6: Social-Cognitive Approach (4 hours)
- Bandura: reciprocal determinism
- Mischel: cognitive-affective personality system (CAPS)
- Person × Situation interactions
- Self-efficacy (Bandura)
- Cross-situational consistency debate (Mischel's critique)

### Topic 7: Personality Assessment (5 hours)
- Self-report inventories (NEO-PI-R, MMPI, 16PF)
- Projective tests (Rorschach, TAT) — controversial reliability
- Behavioral observation
- Implicit measures (IAT)
- Reliability and validity in personality assessment
- Faking, social desirability

### Topic 8: Personality Across the Lifespan (3 hours)
- Stability vs. change
- Rank-order consistency vs. mean-level change
- The "maturity principle" (increasing C, A, decreasing N with age)
- Big Five stability coefficients (~0.7–0.8 in adulthood)
- Critical periods for change

### Topic 9: Personality and Culture (3 hours)
- Cross-cultural validity of Big Five
- Individualism vs. collectivism
- East-West differences in self-concept
- Indigenous personality concepts (e.g., amae in Japan, interdependent self)
- Personality and acculturation

### Topic 10: Personality Neuroscience (3 hours)
- Brain-based personality theories (Eysenck: cortical arousal)
- Neurotransmitters and personality (dopamine → extraversion)
- Personality and the resting brain (default mode network)
- Genetics of personality (twin studies; GWAS)
- Behavioral genetics of the Big Five (heritability ~0.4–0.6)

### Topic 11: Personality in Context (3 hours)
- Personality and work performance (conscientiousness predictor)
- Personality and relationships (partner similarity)
- Personality and health (Type D personality, hostility)
- Personality and risk-taking (Gray's BIS/BAS)
- Personality and political attitudes

### Topic 12: Contemporary Issues (2 hours)
- Dark triad (Machiavellianism, narcissism, psychopathy)
- Personality disorders (link to PSYC7319)
- Personality change interventions
- Digital phenotyping (personality from smartphone data)
- Privacy and ethical concerns

## Key Concepts Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Personality | Enduring patterns of thoughts, feelings, and behaviors that characterize a person |
| Trait | Stable tendency to behave in a particular way across situations |
| Big Five / FFM | Five broad personality dimensions: Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism |
| Factor analysis | Statistical technique identifying clusters of related items |
| Lexical hypothesis | Important personality traits are encoded in language |
| Self-concept | A person's beliefs about themselves, including attributes and roles |
| Self-efficacy | Belief in one's ability to succeed in a specific situation |
| Reciprocal determinism | Interaction of person, behavior, and environment (Bandura) |
| Person × Situation | Interaction between individual differences and contextual factors |
| Nomothetic | Approach that studies general laws across people |
| Idiographic | Approach that studies the unique individual in depth |
| Defense mechanism | Unconscious process that protects the ego from anxiety |
| Projective test | Assessment that asks person to interpret ambiguous stimuli |

## Self-Check Questions

1. Compare Freud's psychodynamic theory with the Big Five trait approach. What are the strengths and limitations of each?
2. Describe the Big Five. Give an example of behavior that would be high vs. low on each dimension.
3. Why did trait psychology move from many specific traits to the Big Five? What role did factor analysis play?
4. Mischel criticized trait theory for ignoring situation. How did the social-cognitive approach respond?
5. Compare self-report and projective measures of personality. What are the trade-offs in reliability and validity?
6. Discuss the stability of personality across the lifespan. What changes? What stays the same?
7. What is the "maturity principle"? What evidence supports it? Are there exceptions?
8. Personality is about 40–60% heritable. What does this mean for personal responsibility and self-change?
9. Compare Western and East Asian conceptions of self. How do they shape personality expression?
10. The Dark Triad (Machiavellianism, narcissism, psychopathy) shares a common factor of callous manipulation. Discuss the implications for personality theory.

## Connection to Capstone

Personality psychology is **highly relevant** for your capstone if you choose:

- **JDM subfield:** Big Five predicts decision styles (especially Conscientiousness → analytical; Openness → creative)
- **Health Psych:** Conscientiousness is the strongest personality predictor of health behaviors and longevity
- **Educational Psych:** Big Five predicts academic performance and learning strategies
- **Quant Psych:** Big Five inventories are classic measurement instruments (factor analysis, reliability)

**Strong fit topics:**
- "Personality and decision-making style in engineering professionals" (uses Big Five)
- "Self-regulation and adherence in solo athletic training" (conscientiousness + self-control)
