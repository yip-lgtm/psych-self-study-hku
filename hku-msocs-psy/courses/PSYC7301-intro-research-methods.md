# PSYC7301 — Introduction to Research Methods in Psychology

## Course Info

- **Code:** PSYC7301
- **Title:** Introduction to research methods in psychology
- **MSocSc(Psy) Compulsory:** Yes (one of 8)
- **BPsych Equiv:** PSYC1004 + PSYC2060 (foundational + intermediate)
- **Self-Study Month:** 1–5 (spine — built across all months)
- **Estimated Study Hours:** 60–80 hours (one of two research spine courses)

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this course, you should be able to:

1. Distinguish scientific from pseudo-scientific approaches to behavior
2. Formulate testable research questions and hypotheses
3. Identify appropriate research designs for different questions
4. Operationalize psychological constructs
5. Apply basic sampling methods and assess sampling bias
6. Recognize threats to internal and external validity
7. Read and critically evaluate published research
8. Apply basic ethical principles to research design
9. Communicate research findings in standard scientific format
10. Identify and assess measurement reliability and validity

## Topic Outline

### Topic 1: What is Science? (3 hours)
- Scientific method vs. pseudoscience
- Falsifiability (Popper)
- Paradigms and paradigm shifts (Kuhn)
- The replication crisis in psychology (2010s–present)
- Open Science movement

### Topic 2: The Research Process (3 hours)
- Generating research questions (observation, theory, prior research)
- Literature search strategies (Google Scholar, PsycINFO, PsyArXiv)
- Hypothesis formulation (directional vs. non-directional)
- Variables: IV, DV, moderator, mediator, control

### Topic 3: Measurement and Operationalization (5 hours)
- Conceptual vs. operational definitions
- Levels of measurement (nominal, ordinal, interval, ratio)
- Reliability (test-retest, internal consistency, inter-rater)
- Validity (content, criterion, construct, face)
- Self-report, behavioral, physiological measures
- Response biases (acquiescence, social desirability, demand characteristics)

### Topic 4: Research Designs I — Experimental (8 hours)
- True experiments: IV manipulation, random assignment, control
- Between-subjects vs. within-subjects designs
- Pre-experimental designs (one-shot, pretest-posttest)
- Quasi-experiments (nonequivalent control group, interrupted time series)
- Field experiments vs. lab experiments

### Topic 5: Research Designs II — Correlational and Observational (5 hours)
- Correlational research (positive, negative, zero)
- Correlation ≠ causation
- Longitudinal vs. cross-sectional
- Observational methods (naturalistic, structured, participant)
- Archival research

### Topic 6: Research Designs III — Qualitative and Mixed Methods (4 hours)
- Qualitative: interviews, focus groups, ethnography
- Mixed methods designs
- When to use qualitative (exploration, meaning, lived experience)
- Thematic analysis basics

### Topic 7: Sampling (4 hours)
- Probability sampling (random, stratified, cluster, systematic)
- Non-probability sampling (convenience, snowball, purposive)
- Sample size considerations (power analysis intro)
- Generalizability and external validity

### Topic 8: Research Ethics (5 hours)
- Historical abuses (Milgram, Stanford Prison, Tuskegee)
- IRB / ethics review process
- Informed consent
- Right to withdraw
- Confidentiality and anonymity
- Vulnerable populations
- CITI Program training (required for research)

### Topic 9: Validity and Confounds (5 hours)
- Internal validity threats (history, maturation, testing, instrumentation)
- External validity threats (population, ecological, temporal)
- Construct validity (Cronbach's α, factor analysis intro)
- Statistical conclusion validity

### Topic 10: Reading and Writing Research (8 hours)
- 3-pass reading method
- IMRaD structure (Introduction, Method, Results, Discussion)
- APA 7th edition format
- Citation management (Zotero)
- Critical evaluation of research

### Topic 11: Descriptive Statistics (5 hours)
- Measures of central tendency (mean, median, mode)
- Measures of variability (SD, range, IQR)
- Distributions (normal, skewed, bimodal)
- Z-scores and standardization

### Topic 12: Introduction to Inferential Statistics (5 hours)
- Sampling distributions
- Standard error
- Confidence intervals
- Logic of hypothesis testing
- Type I vs. Type II errors
- p-values, α level, effect size

## Key Concepts Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Construct validity | The degree to which a test measures the theoretical construct it claims to |
| Internal validity | The degree to which a study can demonstrate a causal relationship |
| External validity | The degree to which findings generalize to other settings/people/times |
| Operationalization | Defining a theoretical construct in measurable terms |
| Random assignment | Allocating participants to conditions by chance, ensuring equivalence |
| Confound | An extraneous variable that systematically varies with the IV |
| Demand characteristics | Cues that signal the study's purpose to participants |
| Floor/ceiling effects | When scores cluster at the bottom/top of a scale, limiting variance |
| Hawthorne effect | Behavior changes when people know they're being observed |
| Placebo effect | Improvement from belief in treatment, not the treatment itself |
| Single-blind | Participant doesn't know condition; double-blind: neither does researcher |
| Pilot study | Small-scale preliminary study to test methods |

## Self-Check Questions

1. Explain the difference between a true experiment and a quasi-experiment. Give a psychology example of each.
2. Why is correlation not causation? Construct a correlational example that might be misinterpreted as causal.
3. A researcher wants to study whether sleep affects memory. What's the IV, DV, and at least 2 potential confounds?
4. Define internal validity and name 3 threats. For each, suggest a control.
5. What's the difference between reliability and validity? Can a measure be valid but not reliable? Vice versa?
6. Explain informed consent. What are the 5 required elements?
7. What's the replication crisis? What 3 reforms have psychology adopted to address it?
8. A study has α = 0.05, p = 0.04, n = 30. Is the effect significant? What about effect size?
9. Describe 3 sampling methods. When would you use each?
10. Explain the 3-pass reading method. Why is it more efficient than linear reading?

## Connection to Capstone

This course is the **foundation of your entire research capability**. Apply it directly:
- **Formulating your research question** (Topic 2)
- **Choosing your design** (Topic 4–6) — for a survey-based capstone, this is correlational
- **Operationalizing your variables** (Topic 3) — selecting validated scales
- **Sampling your N** (Topic 7) — power analysis to justify sample size
- **Ethics review** (Topic 8) — your CITI certificate + informed consent

**Recommended capstone reading:** "How to Read a Paper" by Keshav (2007) for the 3-pass method.

## Sample Discussion Questions

- A study claims to show that "personality causes career success." What 3 questions would you ask to evaluate this claim?
- Why do psychology journals now require pre-registration? How does this improve research quality?
- A friend says they "don't trust psychology research because every study contradicts the last one." How would you respond?
- Compare and contrast: between-subjects vs. within-subjects design. What are the trade-offs?
- The Hawthorne effect is named after a study where workers' productivity increased when they knew they were observed. How would you design a study to detect this effect today?
