# PSYC7305 — Developmental Psychology

## Course Info

- **Code:** PSYC7305
- **Title:** Developmental psychology
- **MSocSc(Psy) Compulsory:** Yes (one of 8)
- **BPsych Equiv:** PSYC2009
- **Self-Study Month:** 3
- **Estimated Study Hours:** 35–45 hours

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this course, you should be able to:

1. Describe major theories of development (Piaget, Vygotsky, Erikson, attachment theory)
2. Explain physical, cognitive, and socioemotional development across the lifespan
3. Discuss the role of nature, nurture, and their interaction in development
4. Identify critical periods and sensitive periods
5. Apply developmental psychology to education, parenting, and policy
6. Critically evaluate research methods in developmental science
7. Discuss cultural influences on development
8. Connect development to other subfields (cognitive, social, personality)

## Topic Outline

### Topic 1: Introduction to Developmental Psychology (2 hours)
- Lifespan perspective
- Nature vs. nurture (interactionist view)
- Continuity vs. discontinuity
- Critical vs. sensitive periods
- Research methods (longitudinal, cross-sectional, sequential)

### Topic 2: Prenatal Development and Infancy (4 hours)
- Conception, germinal, embryonic, fetal stages
- Teratogens (alcohol, drugs, infections)
- Newborn reflexes and capacities
- Sensory and perceptual development
- Temperament (Thomas & Chess: easy, difficult, slow-to-warm-up)

### Topic 3: Cognitive Development I — Piaget (5 hours)
- Piaget's theory: constructivism
- Stages: sensorimotor, preoperational, concrete operational, formal operational
- Key concepts: schemas, assimilation, accommodation, equilibration
- Object permanence (Baillargeon's violation-of-expectation)
- Critiques: underestimates infant competence; cross-cultural limitations

### Topic 4: Cognitive Development II — Vygotsky and Information Processing (4 hours)
- Vygotsky's sociocultural theory
- Zone of proximal development (ZPD)
- Scaffolding
- Private speech
- Information-processing approach
- Core knowledge theories (Spelke)

### Topic 5: Language Development (3 hours)
- Stages: cooing, babbling, one-word, two-word, telegraphic
- Nativist (Chomsky: LAD) vs. learning theories
- Social-pragmatic (Tomasello: intention reading)
- Bilingual development
- Language and thought (Sapir-Whorf hypothesis)

### Topic 6: Attachment Theory (5 hours)
- Bowlby's ethological theory
- Attachment figures and behaviors
- Ainsworth's Strange Situation
- Attachment patterns: secure, avoidant, ambivalent, disorganized
- Internal working models
- Attachment across the lifespan
- Continuity to adult attachment (Hazan & Shaver)
- Cross-cultural variations (Tronick's still-face, German vs. Japanese samples)

### Topic 7: Social and Emotional Development (3 hours)
- Erikson's psychosocial stages (8 stages across lifespan)
- Self-concept development
- Emotional regulation
- Theory of mind
- Moral development: Piaget → Kohlberg → Gilligan
- Prosocial behavior and empathy

### Topic 8: Adolescence (3 hours)
- Puberty and biological changes
- Cognitive changes (formal operational thinking)
- Identity vs. role confusion (Erikson)
- Peer relationships
- Risk-taking and the brain (prefrontal cortex vs. limbic system)
- Storm and stress myth (Arnett's argument)

### Topic 9: Adulthood (3 hours)
- Emerging adulthood (18–25)
- Early adulthood: intimacy vs. isolation
- Career, relationships, generativity
- Midlife: generativity vs. stagnation
- The midlife crisis (research evidence is mixed)
- Stress and coping

### Topic 10: Late Adulthood and Aging (3 hours)
- Physical and cognitive changes
- Socioemotional selectivity theory (Carstensen)
- Wisdom and expertise
- Dementia (Alzheimer's, vascular)
- Death and dying (Kübler-Ross stages — but criticized)
- Successful aging

### Topic 11: Contexts of Development (3 hours)
- Family systems
- Peer influences
- School and education
- Media and technology
- Cultural contexts (individualism vs. collectivism)
- Socioeconomic status and development

### Topic 12: Applications and Contemporary Issues (2 hours)
- Early childhood education (Head Start, Perry Preschool)
- Parenting programs (Triple P, Incredible Years)
- Educational policy
- Cross-cultural psychology of development
- Technology and development (screen time, social media)

## Key Concepts Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Schema | Mental framework for organizing and interpreting information |
| Assimilation | Incorporating new information into existing schemas |
| Accommodation | Modifying schemas to fit new information |
| Equilibration | Process of balancing assimilation and accommodation |
| ZPD (Zone of Proximal Development) | Range of tasks a learner can do with help but not alone |
| Scaffolding | Temporary support provided to help a learner succeed |
| Object permanence | Understanding that objects exist when not seen |
| Theory of mind | Understanding that others have their own thoughts, beliefs, desires |
| Attachment | Lasting psychological bond between infant and caregiver |
| Secure attachment | Infant uses caregiver as secure base for exploration |
| Internal working model | Mental representation of self and others formed in early relationships |
| Critical period | Time window during which certain development must occur |
| Sensitive period | Optimal window for development, but not strictly required |
| Temperament | Innate behavioral style (e.g., inhibited, exuberant) |
| Conservation | Understanding that quantity stays the same despite appearance changes |

## Self-Check Questions

1. Compare Piaget's and Vygotsky's theories of cognitive development. What are the main differences?
2. Describe the four attachment patterns. What parenting behaviors tend to produce each?
3. What is the Zone of Proximal Development? Give a concrete teaching example.
4. Explain the strange situation procedure. What does it measure?
5. Piaget believed infants lacked object permanence. What evidence has challenged this view?
6. Compare Kohlberg's and Gilligan's theories of moral development. How do they differ in emphasis?
7. What does it mean to say adolescence is a "critical period" for social development? What evidence supports this?
8. Describe the "still-face" experiment. What does it reveal about early social development?
9. Discuss the role of culture in development. How would a collectivist culture shape developmental trajectories differently from an individualist one?
10. Evaluate the concept of "emerging adulthood" as a distinct developmental stage. What are the arguments for and against?

**For your multi-domain self-learning context** (educational psych angle), relevant questions:
- How do adult self-learners develop metacognitive skills?

- How does identity develop in polymaths vs. specialists?

## Sample Discussion Questions

- The "nature vs. nurture" debate is often presented as either/or. Why is this misleading?
- Some cultures emphasize formal schooling for young children; others emphasize free play. What developmental theory supports each approach?
- Discuss the evidence for continuity vs. discontinuity in personality across the lifespan.
- Critically evaluate: "Adolescence is inevitably a time of storm and stress." Use research evidence.
- How would you design a study to test whether bilingual education enhances cognitive development?
