# The 3-Pass Paper Reading Method

> From Keshav, S. (2007). "How to Read a Paper." *ACM SIGCOMM Computer Communication Review*, 37(3), 83–84. Adapted for psychology.

---

## Why 3-Pass?

Most beginners read a paper linearly, like a textbook chapter. This is **slow** and produces poor retention. The 3-pass method gives you:

- **Pass 1:** The 5-minute bird's-eye view (is this paper worth my time?)
- **Pass 2:** The 30-minute understanding (can I explain the method and findings?)
- **Pass 3:** The 60-minute deep dive (can I critique and contextualize?)

Allocate your time based on the paper's importance:
- **Trivial / not relevant** → 1 pass, 5 min
- **Relevant to capstone** → 2 passes, 35 min
- **Foundational for your subfield** → 3 passes, 95 min

---

## Pass 1 — Bird's-Eye (5 min)

Read in this order:
1. **Title + abstract** (1 min) — what did they do, find, conclude?
2. **Section headings** (1 min) — how is the paper structured?
3. **Conclusion** (1 min) — do they claim what they set out to?
4. **Introduction** (skim, 2 min) — what's the gap they're filling?

### Questions to answer
- Category: empirical / review / theoretical / methodological?
- Context: which subfield? which ongoing debate?
- Take-away: what's the ONE thing I should remember?
- Relevance: 1–5, should I do Pass 2?

**If relevance ≤ 2, stop here.** Not every paper deserves your time.

---

## Pass 2 — Understand (30 min)

Read with the goal of being able to **explain the paper to a smart friend** in 5 minutes.

### Focus areas
- **Introduction** (5 min): research question, hypotheses, contribution
- **Method** (10 min): participants, materials, procedure, design
- **Results** (10 min): key stats, effect sizes, figures
- **Discussion** (5 min): interpretation, limitations, future directions

### Don't get stuck on
- Statistical formulas (you can look them up later)
- Every detail of the procedure
- Exhaustive note-taking

### After Pass 2
You should be able to:
- State the research question
- Describe the method in 3 sentences
- Summarize the key result with numbers
- State the main limitation
- Connect it to at least 1 other paper you've read

**Write a 150-word "paper card" in your Zotero notes.**

---

## Pass 3 — Reconstruct & Critique (60 min)

This is where you read the paper **as if you were a reviewer**.

### Focus areas
- **The argument:** Is the logic tight? Are claims supported by data?
- **The method:** Sample size justified? Control conditions appropriate? Confounds?
- **The analysis:** Stats appropriate? Effect sizes reported? Multiple comparisons handled?
- **The conclusion:** Do they over-claim? What would change your interpretation?
- **The contribution:** What's the actual advance? Replicable? Generalizable?

### Ask yourself
- What would I do differently if I were running this study?
- What's the strongest alternative explanation for their findings?
- How does this fit with / contradict related work?
- If the result is real, what's the next obvious study?
- Could I build a capstone on this? Extend it?

## Common Paper Reading Traps

### ❌ "I need to understand every word"
**Reality:** You don't. Skip jargon, look it up only if it's central.

### ❌ "I'll re-read it later"
**Reality:** You won't. Take notes now or lose it.

### ❌ "I should highlight everything"
**Reality:** Highlighting is passive. Write a 1-sentence summary per section instead.

### ❌ "Linear reading = careful reading"
**Reality:** Skim first, then deep-read. Don't waste time on Pass 3 if Pass 1 killed it.
