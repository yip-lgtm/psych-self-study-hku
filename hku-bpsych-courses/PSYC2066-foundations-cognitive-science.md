# PSYC2066 — Foundations of Cognitive Science

## Course Info (from HKU Catalog)

- **Code:** PSYC2066
- **Title:** Foundations of cognitive science
- **Credits:** 6
- **HKU Catalog Description:** This course allows students to gain an understanding of the workings of the mind in the context of the technological advances that are increasingly shaping our lives and our society. The course introduces students to the domain, goals and methods of Cognitive Science, showing how different disciplines converge in their enquiry into how the brain works. Lectures will present case studies highlighting research findings which show how similar questions about the functioning of the human mind are answered from the perspective of each contributing discipline.
- **Prerequisites:** COMP1117 or ENGG1330 or LING1000 or PHIL1012 or PSYC1001
- **Assessment:** 100% coursework
- **Notes:** Multidisciplinary; prerequisite for PSYC2067

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this course, you should be able to:

1. Describe cognitive science as an interdisciplinary field
2. Discuss contributions from psychology, AI, linguistics, philosophy, neuroscience
3. Apply multidisciplinary approaches to mind and intelligence
4. Analyze case studies at the intersection of disciplines
5. Discuss the implications of cognitive science for technology and society

## Topic Outline

### Topic 1: Introduction to Cognitive Science (3 hours)
- History (1956 Dartmouth conference)
- The "cognitive revolution"
- Interdisciplinary nature
- Methodological pluralism

### Topic 2: Philosophy of Mind (4 hours)
- Mind-body problem
- Dualism vs. monism
- Functionalism
- Consciousness (hard problem, Chalmers)
- Philosophy of perception

### Topic 3: Psychology Contributions (4 hours)
- Information processing
- Mental representations
- Cognitive architectures (ACT-R, SOAR)
- Connectionism

### Topic 4: Linguistics Contributions (4 hours)
- Chomsky and Universal Grammar
- Formal language theory
- Computational linguistics
- Semantics and pragmatics

### Topic 5: Neuroscience Contributions (4 hours)
- Brain mapping
- Cognitive neuroscience methods
- Neural networks
- Embodied cognition

### Topic 6: Artificial Intelligence (4 hours)
- History (Turing test, symbolic AI)
- Machine learning basics
- Neural networks and deep learning
- Modern AI (LLMs, multimodal)
- AI and consciousness debates

### Topic 7: Anthropology Contributions (3 hours)
- Cross-cultural cognition
- Embodied cognition in context
- Distributed cognition

### Topic 8: Case Studies (4 hours)
- Vision (psychology + neuroscience + AI)
- Language (linguistics + AI + psych)
- Decision making (economics + psych + AI)
- Consciousness (philosophy + neuroscience + AI)

### Topic 9: Embodied and Situated Cognition (3 hours)
- 4E cognition: embodied, embedded, enacted, extended
- Robotics and cognition
- Dynamical systems approach

### Topic 10: Implications for Society (3 hours)
- AI ethics
- Human enhancement
- Cognitive liberty
- Future of cognitive science

## Key Concepts Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Cognitive science | Interdisciplinary study of mind and intelligence |
| Functionalism | Mental states are defined by their function |
| Computationism | Mind as information processing |
| Connectionism | Neural network approach |
| Embodied cognition | Mind shaped by body |
| 4E cognition | Embodied, embedded, enacted, extended |
| Turing test | Behavioral test for machine intelligence |
| Chinese Room | Searle's argument against strong AI |
| Hard problem | Subjective experience (Chalmers) |

## Self-Check Questions

1. What are the 6 disciplines of cognitive science? How does each contribute?
2. Explain functionalism. What are its strengths and weaknesses?
3. Discuss Searle's Chinese Room argument.
4. What is embodied cognition? How does it challenge classical cognitive science?
5. How has AI advanced cognitive science (and vice versa)?
